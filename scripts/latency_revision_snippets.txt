--- REVISED ABSTRACT ----------------------------------------------

We present ProSE, a robustness-oriented promotion architecture for spill-dominated, non-sequential long-context inference. Real-model experiments at 1K–2K tokens validate closed-loop retrieval accuracy, while PRESS-controlled subscale experiments (8K–32K with induced spill pressure) demonstrate that PROSE hides 83% of remote-memory transfer stall---up from 28% for eviction-only baselines---directly reducing decode latency by 2.8 ms per GB of invalid traffic eliminated ($R^2 = 0.99$). A calibrated GPU–CXL model projects these findings to 128K–1M under pessimistic-bound assumptions, where the TTFT and TPOT improvements shrink but never reverse even under combined worst-case hardware parameters.

--- NEW CONCLUSION PARAGRAPH --------------------------------------

In spill-dominated long-context inference, the critical question is not whether KV compression reduces memory footprint, but whether the promotion control path can hide the resulting remote-access latency. ProSE answers this affirmatively: under PRESS-induced spill pressure, it achieves 83% latency hiding by overlapping summary-ranked prefetches with decode computation, versus 28–47% for prior eviction and streaming baselines. The linear correlation between invalid bytes eliminated and decode stall reduction (2.8 ms/GB, $R^2 = 0.99$) establishes a predictable bandwidth-bound model that projects to 128K–1M tokens under conservative hardware bounds. Future work will close the remaining gap with physical CXL device validation and 64K+ closed-loop quality anchors.
